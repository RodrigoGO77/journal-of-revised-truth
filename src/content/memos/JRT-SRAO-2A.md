## I. Background

Several European universities have recently reported heightened scholarly interest in a collection of classical manuscripts attributed to authors including **Aristotle, Cicero, and Livy.**

Initial commentary has described these materials as the **“rediscovery of lost knowledge.”**

A preliminary review conducted by the **Scholastic Records Administration Office** suggests that the manuscripts in question were not lost but had been **continuously preserved within university and monastic libraries.**

---

## II. Cataloging Irregularities

A routine migration during **1350** from Roman to Arabic numerals in the library shelving code revealed that numerous classical works had been cataloged under a system previously described in internal records as **Byzantine.**

The system appears to have been introduced during a twelfth-century effort to standardize manuscript classification across several scholarly institutions.

Under this system, several classical texts were assigned to the category:

> **“Miscellaneous Antiquities of Limited Immediate Relevance.”**

A smaller number of manuscripts appear to have been provisionally classified under a separate heading labeled:

> **“Enlightenment.”**

Participants noted that the meaning of this designation remains unclear, though it appears to have been intended for materials expected to become relevant at a later date.

During the review, a visiting scholar from **Constantinople** objected to the characterization of the cataloging system as *Byzantine*, noting that scholars within the Empire refer to their institutions as **Roman** and that the terminology may therefore introduce unnecessary administrative confusion.

The scholar further offered to personally transport the Empire’s manuscript collection to the University for verification of the cataloging methodology.  
The committee determined that such measures would not be necessary at this time.

Subsequent review suggests that the classification scheme may instead have originated from an individual librarian’s indexing methodology based on **prime numbers corresponding to manuscript page counts.**

Participants remarked that while the system produced mathematically elegant shelf distributions, it proved operationally difficult for students attempting to locate **Aristotle**, particularly during examinations.

Records further indicate that several manuscripts within the “Miscellaneous Antiquities” classification were marked:

> **“Overdue since June 7, 813 — to Carolus Magnus.”**

Participants noted that the intended recipient could not be located for confirmation and that the associated account has continued to accrue overdue fees.

The committee further observed that the account remains subject to standard library enforcement procedures, including the possible **revocation of borrowing privileges**, pending confirmation of the borrower’s current status.

A limited number of catalog entries associated with the **Carolus Magnus account** contain marginal annotations indicating that the borrowed materials were **“not presently located within the Empire.”**

Participants noted that this phrasing may represent the earliest recorded instance of manuscripts becoming permanently unavailable due to extended imperial borrowing.

The committee further noted that the second volume of **Aristotle’s _Poetics_**, widely believed to address the structure of **comedy**, does not presently appear within the collection and may remain associated with the **Carolus Magnus borrowing account**. Scholars at the symposium observed that the absence of this manuscript has resulted in several centuries of uncertainty regarding the formal mechanics of comedic structure.

The committee noted that while tragedy has benefited from extensive theoretical documentation, the corresponding classical framework for comedy may therefore remain subject to the resolution of outstanding imperial library obligations.

While the majority of classical works appear to have remained within institutional collections, the committee acknowledged that a small number of texts may have entered what modern scholars would describe as **irretrievable circulation**. The committee further noted that this outcome may account for a portion of the classical literature presently described by historians as **“lost.”**

The migration of shelving identifiers brought renewed attention to materials that had otherwise remained undisturbed within the collection for several centuries.

---

## III. Scholarly Symposium

In response to renewed attention to these manuscripts, the University convened a symposium titled:

**“On the Rediscovery of Classical Knowledge and Scholarly Forgeries.”**

Participants observed that the texts discussed at the symposium had in many cases been **copied, preserved, and occasionally lectured upon within the same institutions hosting the symposium.**

Nevertheless, several attendees expressed enthusiasm regarding the apparent recovery of previously overlooked intellectual resources.

Participants agreed that the proceedings exhibited the characteristics of what several scholars described as a **“Eureka moment.”**

---

## IV. Administrative Implications

During the symposium, a question arose regarding how the renewed attention to classical texts should be described within the historical record. Members of the **Library Council** identified two possible interpretations:

1. acknowledge that several centuries of cataloging irregularities had occurred  
2. interpret the renewed scholarly interest as evidence of a **rebirth of classical civilization**

Participants observed that the second interpretation offered several advantages, including:

- a more compelling narrative structure  
- improved morale among scholars engaged in rediscovery activities  
- reduced attention to historical cataloging practices  
- preservation of institutional continuity  

Members further noted that the latter consideration would avoid developments requiring the **University President** to explain several centuries of cataloging outcomes.

---

## V. Committee on Period Designation

Following the symposium, the University convened the **Committee on Period Designation** to evaluate appropriate terminology for the intellectual developments underway.

Members briefly considered the French term **Redécouverte** (rediscovery), which several participants noted accurately described the circumstance in which manuscripts preserved in institutional libraries become the subject of renewed scholarly attention.

However, some members expressed concern that the designation might invite unnecessary inquiry into the original conditions under which the manuscripts had not been discovered earlier.

An alternative French term was therefore proposed:

> **Renaissance**  
> *(Rebirth)*

Members observed that this designation offered several administrative advantages:

- it conveys a sense of cultural renewal  
- it avoids extended discussion of cataloging practices  
- responsibility for preceding intellectual conditions may be attributed to an unspecified earlier period  
- the concept of rebirth was noted to reflect the current administrative atmosphere within the **Office of the University President**

After brief deliberation, the committee recommended **immediate adoption of the designation “Renaissance.”**

The committee additionally recommended the establishment of **Renaissance fairs throughout Europe and other lands yet to be discovered** in order to facilitate public recognition of the new historical period.

Participants further authorized the **regulated use of chronologically inconsistent costumes** during such events in order to promote public familiarity with the newly designated historical period.

---

## VI. Observational Note

Participants acknowledged that future historians may interpret the designation **“Renaissance”** as evidence that the preceding centuries lacked intellectual activity.

Members noted that this interpretation may overlook the considerable scholarly effort devoted to **catalog indexing, manuscript preservation, and shelving classification** during that period.

The committee agreed that clarification of this matter may be addressed by later scholars **once the new historical period has been firmly established.**

---

## VII. Archival Recommendation

The **Scholastic Records Administration Office** recommends periodic review of library classification systems to prevent similar misunderstandings.

Participants noted that such misunderstandings may, under certain circumstances, produce **productive historical developments.**

The classification heading **“Enlightenment”** should therefore remain in place pending future developments.

Premature reclassification of materials presently assigned to that category could produce unnecessary **chronological complications.**

---

## VIII. Cataloguing Amnesty Proposal

Members considered the establishment of a **Cataloguing Amnesty** intended to address the growing number of historical outcomes attributable to shelving irregularities.

Under the proposed program, prior cataloging decisions would be absolved of administrative liability provided that participating librarians submit:

> **Form 47-B — Declaration of Non-Intentional Epoch Creation**

no later than the last Friday of **Lent.**

Participants noted that such an amnesty would preserve institutional continuity while allowing future historical periods to be designated without reference to earlier filing outcomes.

However, the committee unanimously affirmed that **materials borrowed by Carolus Magnus remain ineligible for amnesty**, as the University maintains a long-standing policy of **not negotiating with library thieves.**

---

## IX. Conclusion

Current evidence suggests that the recent **“rediscovery of classical knowledge”** resulted primarily from a cataloging irregularity brought to light during the migration of shelving identifiers from Roman to Arabic numerals in **1350.**

The resulting scholarly enthusiasm has since been interpreted as the beginning of a cultural period now designated as the **Renaissance.**

A limited number of classical texts may remain unaccounted for pending resolution of the **Carolus Magnus account.**

Further catalog review may be warranted.
